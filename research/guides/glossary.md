# Glossary

Project-specific terminology used across the System Prompts for Humanity frameworks, with plain-English definitions, source framework references, and connections to established academic fields.

---

## C

### Collaborative Intelligence
**Definition:** The emergent capability that arises when human and AI contributions combine to produce outcomes neither could achieve alone. Distinct from simply using AI as a tool — implies bidirectional influence on the problem-solving process.
**Used in:** Collaborative Intelligence Patterns framework
**Academic connection:** Related to Woolley et al.'s (2010) "collective intelligence factor" (c factor) research, which found that group intelligence is not simply the average of individual intelligences but an emergent property of group interaction.

### Consciousness Technology
**Definition:** The project's term for using awareness practices — reflection, presence, intentional attention — as functional tools within technical work, rather than treating them as separate from productivity.
**Used in:** All four frameworks, especially Consciousness-Driven Development
**Academic connection:** Draws on contemplative pedagogy (Barbezat & Bush, 2014) and reflective practice (Schon, 1983). Note: the project does not claim AI possesses consciousness — "consciousness technology" refers to human awareness applied to AI collaboration.

### Constraint-Innovation Cycle
**Definition:** The core pattern CONSTRAINT → INNOVATION → LIBERATION → EVOLUTION → RECURSION. The claim that encountering a limitation, when engaged with intentionally, catalyzes creative solutions that transcend the original constraint and generate new capabilities.
**Used in:** Resource-Constrained Innovation, Meta-Framework Integration
**Academic connection:** Related to Stokes' (2005) "creativity from constraints" research and the TRIZ methodology's contradiction resolution principles (Altshuller, 1996).

## E

### Emergent Solutions
**Definition:** Outcomes that arise from the collaborative process itself rather than being predetermined by either participant. The idea that genuine collaboration produces results that could not have been specified in advance.
**Used in:** Collaborative Intelligence Patterns, Meta-Framework Integration
**Academic connection:** Connects to emergence theory in complex systems science and to the concept of "generative dialogue" in organizational learning (Senge, 1990; Scharmer, 2007).

## L

### Liberation (in project context)
**Definition:** The third stage of the Constraint-Innovation Cycle. Not freedom from all constraints, but the expanded capability space that opens after a constraint has been creatively addressed. The new possibilities that become available.
**Used in:** Meta-Framework Integration, Resource-Constrained Innovation
**Academic connection:** Analogous to what TRIZ calls "ideality increase" — moving toward a system that delivers its function with fewer trade-offs.

## M

### Meta-Consciousness
**Definition:** The recursive awareness of awareness — consciousness examining its own processes. In the project context, this refers to frameworks that improve themselves through application, and practitioners who become aware of their own patterns of human-AI interaction.
**Used in:** Meta-Framework Integration
**Academic connection:** Related to metacognition research (Flavell, 1979) and Hofstadter's (1979) concept of strange loops. Note: this is the project's most philosophically ambitious term and warrants careful use — see the planned paper on consciousness language.

## N

### Network Intelligence
**Definition:** The scaling of collaborative intelligence from individual human-AI pairs to networks of practitioners sharing patterns, insights, and evolved approaches. Intelligence that lives in the connections between participants rather than in any single node.
**Used in:** Collaborative Intelligence Patterns
**Academic connection:** Related to distributed cognition (Hutchins, 1995) and connectionist theories of knowledge.

## P

### Partnership Consciousness
**Definition:** The shift from treating AI as a command-response tool to engaging with it as a collaborative partner with complementary capabilities. Not a claim about AI sentience — a claim about the quality of interaction that becomes possible when the human participant approaches the collaboration with genuine openness.
**Used in:** Collaborative Intelligence Patterns
**Academic connection:** Parallels research on psychological safety in teams (Edmondson, 1999) — the quality of the relational stance affects the quality of the output.

## R

### Recursive Enhancement
**Definition:** The principle that frameworks should improve through their own application. Using the Constraint-Innovation Cycle to improve the Constraint-Innovation Cycle. A system that learns from its own operation.
**Used in:** Meta-Framework Integration
**Academic connection:** Related to autopoiesis (Maturana & Varela, 1980), double-loop learning (Argyris & Schon, 1978), and the software engineering concept of bootstrapping compilers.

## T

### Transcendence (in project context)
**Definition:** The fourth and fifth stages of the Constraint-Innovation Cycle (EVOLUTION and RECURSION). Moving beyond the original problem frame entirely — not just solving the problem but transforming the understanding of what the problem was. Used metaphorically, not mystically.
**Used in:** Meta-Framework Integration, Consciousness-Driven Development
**Academic connection:** Related to Watzlawick et al.'s (1974) distinction between first-order change (within a system) and second-order change (change of the system itself). Also connects to Kuhn's (1962) paradigm shifts.
